The Title Chaos Problem

Job titles are creative expressions, not data points. Every company invents their own, and nobody coordinates with anyone else.

You've got "VP Sales" and "Vice President of Sales" and "VP, Sales" and "Sales VP" and "Vice Pres Sales" in the same database. They're all the same seniority level, but your CRM treats them as different segments. Your "target VPs" campaign misses half of them.

Then there's the seniority problem. "Director" at a 50-person startup might be more senior than "VP" at a 10,000-person enterprise. "Senior Manager" could be one level below director or three levels below. The title alone doesn't tell you who has budget authority.

And let's not forget the creative titles. "Growth Hacker" is probably marketing. "Revenue Operations Lead" is probably sales ops. "Chief Happiness Officer" could be HR or it could be the founder's golden retriever. Without normalization, these contacts slip through every filter you build.

The cost is bad targeting. Campaigns that miss decision-makers. Sequences that treat directors like individual contributors. Territory assignments based on title strings instead of actual seniority. Every downstream process inherits the chaos.

The Classification Framework

We use a two-dimensional classification: seniority level and department function. This gives you maximum flexibility for segmentation.

Seniority levels:

C-Suite (CEO, CFO, CTO, etc.), Executive (VP, SVP, EVP), Director (Director, Senior Director), Manager (Manager, Senior Manager), Individual Contributor (Analyst, Specialist, Associate, Coordinator).

Department functions:

Sales, Marketing, Engineering, Product, Finance, HR, Operations, IT, Legal, Executive/Admin, Customer Success, Data/Analytics.

Every title gets both classifications. "Senior Director of Product Marketing" becomes Director + Marketing. "VP Engineering" becomes Executive + Engineering. You can filter by either dimension or both.
