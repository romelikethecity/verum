The Phone Format Problem

Phone numbers are chaos. The same number gets entered as (555) 123-4567 and 555-123-4567 and 555.123.4567 and 5551234567 and +1 555 123 4567 and 1-555-123-4567. Twelve different representations of identical data.

Your CRM sees them as different numbers. Duplicate detection fails because the text strings don't match. Someone calls a prospect, logs the call, and the activity doesn't show up on the existing contact record because the phone formats differ.

Dialers are worse. Some require E.164 format. Some need ten digits exactly. Some choke on parentheses. When formats are inconsistent, calls fail silently or route incorrectly. SDRs waste time reformatting numbers manually or worse, skip calling entirely because "the number doesn't work."

Then there's the validation problem. Is this number even real? Did someone fat-finger a digit? Is it a valid US number or did they forget the country code? Format standardization without validation just makes bad data look consistent. You need both.

Format Options

We standardize to whatever format your systems need:

E.164 (international standard): +15551234567. No spaces, no punctuation. Maximum compatibility with dialers, APIs, and telephony systems. This is the safest default.

National display format: (555) 123-4567. Readable for humans, common in US databases. Good for display but some systems struggle with it.

International display format: +1 555 123 4567. Readable with country code. Good for global teams.

Custom format: Whatever your CRM or dialer requires. We match the specification.

You can also get multiple formats. E.164 for the system field, display format for a separate human-readable field.
