The Name Data Problem

Names look simple until you try to parse them at scale. You've got "john smith" and "JOHN SMITH" and "Smith, John" and "John R. Smith" and "Dr. John Smith Jr." in the same database. Your email templates need "Hi John" but the data gives you full names, reversed names, or names with random capitalization.

The edge cases are endless. Mary Jane Watson: is Mary Jane the first name, or is Jane Watson a compound surname? Jean-Pierre Martin: hyphenated first name. Kim Park: could be first-last or last-first depending on origin. Maria del Carmen Garcia: Spanish compound name with particle. These aren't rare cases. They're everywhere in any global database.

Then there's the junk that gets entered in name fields. Company names ("John Smith at Acme Corp"). Phone numbers. Email addresses. Random characters from copy-paste errors. People enter whatever fits in the box.

The cost shows up in your outreach. "Hi JOHN" screams automated. "Hi Smith, John" is obviously wrong. "Hi Mary Jane" might be awkward if her name is actually Mary. Every malformed personalization erodes trust before you even get to your message.

What Name Parsing Includes

  • First/last name splitting. "John Smith" becomes John + Smith. "Mary Jane Watson" gets intelligent splitting based on name patterns.
  • Prefix extraction. Dr., Mr., Mrs., Ms., Prof. Pulled out to a separate field so "Hi Dr. Smith" works when appropriate.
  • Suffix handling. Jr., Sr., III, PhD, MD, Esq. Separated from last name so matching and deduplication work correctly.
  • Middle name detection. "John Robert Smith" gives you John + Robert + Smith. Middle initials handled too.
  • Capitalization normalization. "JOHN SMITH" becomes "John Smith". "mcdonald" becomes "McDonald". Special patterns respected.
  • Reversed name detection. "Smith, John" recognized and corrected to first-last order.
  • International name handling. Eastern order, compound surnames, particles (van, von, de). Cultural patterns respected.
  • Junk filtering. Company names, emails, phone numbers, garbage in name fields. Flagged for review or removal.

The Parsing Challenge

Why is this hard? Because names don't follow rules. We use a combination of approaches:

Pattern matching. "Smith, John" has a clear reversed pattern. "John R. Smith" clearly has a middle initial. Many names follow recognizable patterns.

Name dictionaries. Large databases of first names, last names, prefixes, suffixes, and cultural name patterns. When we see "Jean-Pierre," we recognize it as a hyphenated first name.

Contextual signals. Email domain hints at cultural origin. Job title might indicate expected naming pattern. Company location provides context.

Human review. Truly ambiguous cases get manual classification. "Kim Lee" could parse different ways depending on context, and sometimes only human judgment can get it right.
