Healthcare Market Sizing Requires Specialization

Healthcare companies love to cite the total addressable market as some massive slice of $4 trillion in US healthcare spending. Investors are skeptical because they've heard it before. The actual serviceable market for any healthcare technology is orders of magnitude smaller.

Provider fragmentation creates complexity. There are over 6,000 hospitals, 200,000 physician practices, and countless other healthcare organizations. But they don't all buy the same things. A rural critical access hospital has completely different needs than an academic medical center. Your ICP is specific. Your TAM should reflect that.

Payer concentration affects dynamics. Healthcare payers are highly concentrated. A handful of large nationals plus Blues plans plus regional players. Selling to payers is a different motion than selling to providers, with different decision cycles and procurement processes.

Regulatory requirements create barriers. HIPAA compliance, FDA approvals, state licensing variations. Some markets require significant regulatory investment before you can even compete. That affects both market size and time to revenue.

Sales cycles are long and committee-driven. Healthcare organizations make technology decisions slowly, with multiple stakeholders. Your market isn't just the organizations that fit your ICP. It's the ones where you can actually get a decision made in a reasonable timeframe.

Real healthcare market analysis accounts for these factors. Otherwise you're presenting numbers that investors won't believe and that won't help you allocate resources.

What Healthcare Market Analysis Involves

We build market sizing from provider and payer data, not industry spending estimates. How many organizations fit your criteria? What's their technology spend? Where is competition weak?

Provider segmentation. Hospitals, health systems, physician practices, ambulatory centers. By specialty, size, geography, and ownership type.

Payer analysis. Commercial insurers, Medicare contractors, Medicaid managed care. Concentration by market and segment.

Competitive landscape. Who serves each segment? Where are incumbents entrenched? Where are there gaps and emerging opportunities?

Regulatory environment. Compliance requirements, FDA pathways if relevant, state-by-state variations. How regulation affects market entry and competitive position.

Growth and consolidation trends. M&A activity, provider consolidation, technology adoption trends. What's changing in the market?

Common Questions

How do you size healthcare markets?

Provider databases (NPI registry, state licensing), payer enrollment data, organization databases. Bottom-up counting by specialty, facility type, and geography.

Can you segment by provider type?

Absolutely. Hospitals vs practices vs ASCs. Primary care vs specialists. Academic vs community. Each has different buying processes and needs.

What about payer markets?

Yes. Payer segments by type, size, and geography. Concentrated buyers, regulated pricing, complex contracting. Different dynamics from provider markets.

How do you handle regulatory complexity?

HIPAA, FDA if relevant, state variations. We include regulatory landscape analysis and how it affects market entry and competition.
