You Know Your Revenue But Not Your Revenue

Every company knows their total revenue. Most know it by month and quarter. Some know it by product line. Very few actually understand the dynamics underneath the numbers.

Revenue concentration goes unnoticed until it's a crisis. Your top 10 customers might represent 40% of revenue. You won't know until one leaves and the board asks questions. Concentration risk hides in plain sight.

Growth and decline blend into one number. Total revenue grew 15%. Great. But one segment grew 40% while another declined 20%. The growing segment deserves investment. The declining segment needs attention or abandonment. Averages hide both.

New vs expansion vs recurring blur together. Is growth coming from new customers or expansion of existing ones? Is recurring revenue stable or slowly eroding? These questions have different answers and different implications.

Product and customer segment profitability is unknown. You have gross margin numbers. You don't have them by segment. Some customers cost more to serve. Some products have better margins. But nobody's done the math.

Revenue analysis answers the questions that should drive strategy: where is money coming from, where is it going, and what should you do about it?

What Revenue Analysis Involves

We take your transaction data and slice it every way that matters. The goal is understanding composition, dynamics, and risk.

Segment composition. Revenue by customer type, industry, size, geography. Which segments matter most? How is the mix changing?

Revenue type analysis. New business vs expansion vs renewal. One-time vs recurring. How healthy is each component? What's growing and declining?

Concentration analysis. Customer concentration, segment concentration, product concentration. Where are the risks? What happens if key customers leave?

Growth dynamics. Which segments are growing fastest? Which are shrinking? What's driving the change? Is growth sustainable?

Cohort trends. How do customer cohorts perform over time? Do newer customers perform better or worse than older ones? Is the business getting healthier?

The Process

Step 1: Data collection (1-2 days). Transaction-level revenue data with customer and product attributes.

Step 2: Data enrichment (2-3 days). Add firmographic data for customer segmentation. Normalize product categories if needed.

Step 3: Composition analysis (3-4 days). Slice revenue by every relevant dimension. Build segment-level views.

Step 4: Dynamics analysis (2-3 days). Analyze trends, cohorts, growth drivers, and risk factors.

Step 5: Synthesis and delivery. Turn analysis into strategic recommendations. Present findings with implications.

Total timeline: 2-3 weeks from data handoff to final delivery.

Common Questions

What revenue data do you analyze?

Transaction-level data: amount, customer, product, date. We slice by segment, product, geography, channel, and time. Where money comes from and where it's going.

How is this different from finance reports?

Finance shows totals. Analysis explains composition and dynamics. Which segments grow? Where is risk concentrated? Strategic questions finance doesn't address.

Can you identify concentration risks?

Yes. Customer, segment, product, geographic concentration. If too much depends on too few sources, we quantify the risk and identify diversification opportunities.

Do you analyze recurring vs one-time?

Absolutely. Different revenue types have different values. Understanding the mix and how it's changing is critical for planning.
