Your Market Sizing Is Probably Fiction

Most companies have a TAM slide somewhere. Probably came from a Gartner or Forrester report. Some massive number that sounds impressive to investors. But does it actually describe your market?

Industry-wide TAM isn't your TAM. The CRM market might be $80B. But you're not selling to everyone who buys CRM. You have an ICP. You have geographic constraints. You have price points. Your actual addressable market is a fraction of the headline number.

Market segments have wildly different sizes. Healthcare might be 20% of your TAM by revenue potential. Or it might be 5%. Without segment-level analysis, you're allocating resources to segments based on intuition, not opportunity.

Competitive intensity varies by segment. A big market full of strong competitors might be worse than a smaller market with weak ones. Market size without competitive mapping is half the picture.

Growth rates differ dramatically. Some segments are growing 30% annually. Others are flat. Some are declining. Chasing mature markets with growth expectations will disappoint.

Real market analysis starts with your definition of the market, builds from actual company data, and gives you segment-level intelligence you can act on.

What Market Analysis Involves

We size your market from the bottom up. How many companies actually fit your ICP? What's their potential spend? Where are the opportunities?

TAM sizing by segment. Not one number but a matrix. How big is each industry segment? Each company size segment? Each geography? Real numbers built from company data.

Company identification. Who are the companies in your market? Names, not just counts. This becomes a prospecting list, not just a slide.

Competitive mapping. Who else serves these segments? What's their market share? Where is competition intense vs light? Where do you have positioning leverage?

Penetration analysis. What percentage of your TAM have you reached? Which segments have low penetration (room to grow) vs high (need to defend)?

Whitespace identification. Where are there underserved segments? What market opportunities aren't being addressed by competition?
