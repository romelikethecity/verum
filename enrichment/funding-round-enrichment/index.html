The Timing Problem

Two companies look identical on paper. Same industry, same size, same ICP fit. But one just closed a $30M Series B and is hiring 50 people. The other hasn't raised in three years and is running lean. Which one is more likely to buy?

Funding changes everything about how a company operates. Post-funding, there's pressure to grow fast. Hire people, buy tools, expand infrastructure, prove the investment was worth it. That pressure creates buying urgency that doesn't exist at bootstrapped or late-stage companies running on profitability.

The challenge is knowing who just raised. TechCrunch catches the big rounds, but most funding announcements fly under the radar. Your team isn't spending their morning reading Crunchbase alerts. So the insight that could prioritize your best opportunities gets missed.

We've seen sales teams restructure their entire outbound motion around funding signals. Instead of alphabetically working through territory lists, they prioritize recently funded companies. Connect rates go up because these companies are actively building. Close rates go up because they have budget.

What We Provide

  • Funding stage. Seed, Series A, B, C, D, and beyond. Different stages signal different priorities and buying patterns.
  • Last funding date. Know how recent the money came in. A company 6 months post-funding is still in growth mode. Three years post-funding is a different story.
  • Total funding amount. Cumulative capital raised. Indicates the scale of resources available.
  • Last round amount. The most recent raise. A $5M seed is different from a $100M growth round.
  • Investors. Who invested. Tier-1 VCs often push portfolio companies toward specific vendors. Useful context for sales conversations.
  • Funding velocity. Time between rounds. Fast raisers are executing and growing. Slow raisers might be struggling.
  • Public market data. For public companies, market cap, recent stock performance, and relevant financial indicators.
