The Timing Problem

You have a target account list. Maybe a thousand companies, maybe ten thousand. Some of them will buy something like your product this year. Most won't. The question is which ones.

Without buying signals, your outreach is essentially random. Same messaging to companies that just closed a funding round as to companies in a hiring freeze. Same priority for an account that just fired their incumbent vendor as one locked into a three-year contract. You're playing the averages instead of playing the odds.

Companies don't buy on your timeline. They buy on theirs. The funding round closes. The new VP joins. The existing solution fails. The budget gets approved. These moments happen constantly across your target accounts. The companies that know about them first get there first.

We worked with a data protection company that had 50,000 accounts in their database accumulated over 17 years. After signal enrichment, they identified 703 MSP accounts showing active buying signals that had been hiding in generic partner categories. At their average deal size, that's $875K in new ARR potential from accounts already sitting in their CRM.

What Buying Signals Tell You

A buying signal is any change that suggests a company might be ready to purchase. Some signals are obvious. A company announces a $50M Series C and posts 12 new job openings in your buyer persona. Others are subtle. A shift in their technology stack that creates a gap your product fills.

No single signal means a deal is imminent. But when multiple signals cluster together, that pattern tells you something. The company that just raised funding, hired a new VP of Sales, and started researching solutions in your category isn't browsing. They're buying.

Signals We Track

  • Funding announcements. Seed through Series D and beyond. When the money arrives, so does the budget. Fresh funding often means new initiatives and new purchases.
  • Executive hiring. New VPs and C-suite executives typically bring change. A new CMO often means a new marketing stack. A new CRO often means new sales tools.
  • Job postings. What they're hiring for tells you what they're building. A company posting for Salesforce admins is expanding their CRM. A company hiring data engineers is building infrastructure.
  • Technology changes. New tools in their stack create integration needs and displacement opportunities. If they just adopted your competitor, they're in buying mode. If they just dropped them, even better.
  • Company growth. Headcount increases, new office locations, revenue milestones. Growing companies have growing needs.
  • Content consumption. When multiple people at an account research your category, that's intent. When they research your competitors specifically, that's active evaluation.
  • Contract renewals. Most B2B contracts run 1-3 years. Knowing when the incumbent is up for renewal tells you when the window opens.
