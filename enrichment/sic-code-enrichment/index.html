Why SIC Still Matters

NAICS replaced SIC in 1997. You'd think after 25+ years everyone would have migrated. They haven't.

SEC filings still use SIC codes. Public company annual reports, 10-Ks, and financial databases reference SIC. If you're doing research on public companies or integrating with financial data sources, you need SIC.

OSHA and some regulatory databases use SIC. Insurance underwriting often references SIC codes. Many legacy enterprise systems built in the 80s and 90s have SIC baked into their data models and haven't been updated.

Sometimes you just need both systems. NAICS for modern segmentation, SIC for compatibility. We can provide them simultaneously.

Common Questions

What's the difference between SIC and NAICS?

SIC is older (1937), 4-digit codes. NAICS is newer (1997), 6-digit codes with better modern coverage. SIC is still used for regulatory and financial purposes.

Why use SIC instead of NAICS?

SEC filings, OSHA data, financial databases, and legacy systems still use SIC. If you're integrating with these systems, you need SIC codes.

Do you provide secondary SIC codes?

Yes. Companies in multiple industries get primary and secondary codes.

How do you handle newer industries?

SIC was last updated in 1987. Modern companies get mapped to the closest appropriate code, with notes when classification is approximate.
