Why Technology Data Changes Everything

Firmographics tell you who they are. Revenue, employee count, industry. Useful, but incomplete. Two 500-person SaaS companies might have completely different technology needs based on their current stack.

If you sell a Salesforce integration, you need to know who uses Salesforce. If you compete with HubSpot, you need to know who's running HubSpot. If your product requires AWS, you need to know who's on AWS. This isn't nice-to-have context. It's the difference between relevant outreach and wasted effort.

Technology enrichment adds this dimension to your targeting. What CRM do they use? What marketing automation? What analytics platform? What cloud infrastructure? Now your segmentation actually means something. Your messaging connects to their reality.

How Detection Works

Technologies leave traces. Website code reveals tracking scripts, form handlers, and embedded widgets. Job postings mention specific tools. DNS records indicate email providers and hosting. Even SSL certificates provide signals.

We don't rely on one signal. Multiple indicators combined increase confidence in detection accuracy. When we see HubSpot tracking code, HubSpot form handlers, and HubSpot job requirements, we're confident about HubSpot. When we only see one indicator, we flag it as lower confidence.

Some technologies are highly visible (marketing tools, analytics). Others are harder to detect (backend systems, internal tools). We're transparent about what we can and can't see reliably.
