Who Sells to Financial Advisors

Financial advisors spend money on two things: tools that help them serve clients better and services that help them grow their practice. Different products reach different people in advisory firms.

Technology platforms. Portfolio management systems, financial planning software, CRM, client portals, reporting tools. The firm principal or operations lead evaluates these. Technology decisions are sticky once made.

Custodians and asset managers. RIAs need custodians. Asset managers want shelf space. These relationships are cultivated over time with the principal and key advisors.

Compliance and regulatory. Compliance software, regulatory filing services, audit support. The CCO (Chief Compliance Officer) handles these. At small RIAs, that's often the owner wearing another hat.

Marketing services. Websites, content marketing, lead generation, seminar marketing. Most advisors want more clients. Few know how to market effectively. Operations managers or the principal make these decisions.

Practice management consulting. Business coaching, succession planning, M&A advisory. Principals of growing firms want guidance on scaling. Retiring advisors need exit strategies.

Insurance products. E&O insurance, cyber liability, client-facing insurance products for cross-selling. The principal handles firm insurance. Individual advisors may have authority to recommend client products.

Advisor Segments

RIAs (Registered Investment Advisors). Fiduciaries registered with SEC (over $100M AUM) or state regulators (under $100M). Fee-based business model. Regulatory filings provide detailed data.

Broker-dealers. FINRA-registered. Commission-based or hybrid models. Large wirehouses down to independent broker-dealers.

Independent advisors. Solo practitioners or small teams. The advisor is the business. Decisions are quick but budgets are smaller.

Multi-advisor firms. Multiple advisors under common ownership or affiliation. May have dedicated operations staff. Larger deals but longer sales cycles.

By AUM tier. Emerging ($0-100M), established ($100M-500M), large ($500M-1B), enterprise ($1B+). AUM correlates with technology spend and sophistication.

Common Questions

Can you provide AUM data?

For RIAs, yes. SEC and state filings include AUM ranges. We can filter by AUM bands: under $100M, $100M-$500M, $500M-$1B, $1B+.

RIAs vs broker-dealers?

Different registration types. RIAs register as fiduciaries with SEC or states. Broker-dealers register with FINRA. We can filter by type.

What contacts are available?

Firm principal, CCO, operations manager, individual advisors. Small RIAs are one-person shops. Larger firms have department heads.

Can you identify specialty?

Yes. Retirement planning, estate planning, tax planning, business owners, high-net-worth. ADV filings often disclose client types.
