Who Sells to Law Firms

Law firms are professional service businesses with specific vendor needs. They're conservative buyers but spend heavily on what they need. Different products reach different people in the firm.

Practice management software. Case management, document management, time tracking, billing. The managing partner or administrator evaluates these at smaller firms. IT directors handle it at larger ones.

Legal research tools. Beyond Westlaw and Lexis, there's a growing market for specialized research tools. Practice group leaders and individual attorneys influence these decisions.

Marketing and business development. Websites, SEO, content marketing, client development coaching. Marketing directors at mid-size firms, managing partners at smaller ones.

Staffing and recruiting. Legal staffing, paralegal placement, associate recruiting. HR managers at large firms, administrators at smaller ones. Legal talent is competitive and expensive.

Financial services. Trust accounting, client payment processing, practice loans, E&O insurance. The managing partner or CFO handles these decisions.

Office services. Court reporting, process serving, document scanning, legal copying. Operations managers and paralegals use these services regularly.

CLE and training. Continuing legal education, practice management training, technology training. Attorneys need CLE credits. Training companies target individual attorneys and firm administrators.

Law Firm Segments

Solo practitioners. One attorney practices. The attorney is the decision-maker. Quick decisions, limited budgets, high volume opportunity.

Small firms (2-10 attorneys). Partners share overhead and decisions. Often practice-area focused. The managing partner or founding partner decides.

Mid-size firms (11-50 attorneys). May have dedicated administrators, marketing staff, IT support. More formal vendor evaluation processes.

Large firms (50+). Departmental structure, procurement processes, IT departments. Longer sales cycles but larger deals.

By practice area. Personal injury, corporate/M&A, family law, criminal defense, immigration, IP, employment, real estate. Different practice areas have different needs and buying patterns.
