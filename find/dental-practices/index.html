Who Sells to Dental Practices

Dental practices buy constantly. Equipment wears out, supplies run low, technology evolves. If you're selling to dentists, you need a way to reach them efficiently.

Dental equipment and supplies. Chairs, X-ray machines, handpieces, sterilization equipment. The dentist-owner makes major equipment decisions. Office managers handle supplies.

Practice management software. Scheduling, patient records, billing, treatment planning. The dentist or office manager evaluates these. Smaller practices are price-sensitive. Larger groups want integration capabilities.

Patient communication. Appointment reminders, recall systems, patient education, review management. Office managers often drive these purchases.

Financial services. Patient financing, equipment loans, merchant services, practice acquisition lending. Owners need these, especially newer practices still building their patient base.

Marketing services. Websites, SEO, direct mail, social media management. Dentists know they need marketing. Most don't know where to start.

HR and staffing. Dental hygienists, assistants, front office staff. Finding good dental staff is chronically difficult. Staffing agencies target practice owners and office managers.

Insurance and compliance. Malpractice coverage, HIPAA compliance, OSHA training. Required purchases that happen on predictable cycles.

Data Fields Available

  • Dentist/owner contacts. Name, credentials (DDS, DMD), direct email, direct phone when available.
  • Specialty. General dentistry, orthodontics, oral surgery, periodontics, endodontics, pediatric, prosthodontics, cosmetic focus.

Practice Segments

Solo practitioners. One dentist, often owner-operated. The dentist makes all decisions. Straightforward sales process but lower deal values.

Group practices. Multiple dentists sharing space, staff, and overhead. May have dedicated office managers who influence operational purchases.

DSO-affiliated practices. Owned or managed by Dental Service Organizations. Local dentists focus on clinical care while DSO handles business operations. Procurement often happens at the DSO level.

Specialists. Orthodontists, oral surgeons, periodontists, endodontists. Different equipment needs, different patient flow, different economics than general dentists.

By geography. Urban, suburban, rural. Different competitive dynamics and patient demographics.

Common Questions

Can you separate specialists from general dentists?

Yes. Orthodontics, oral surgery, periodontics, endodontics, pediatric, prosthodontics. We identify specialty from licensing boards and practice positioning.

How do you find dentist contacts?

State dental boards are primary. Every practicing dentist has a license on file. We cross-reference with ADA records, practice websites, and business registrations.

What about DSO practices?

We can filter for independent practices only, DSO-affiliated only, or both. DSO affiliation affects who makes purchasing decisions.

What contacts are available?

Dentist/owner, office manager, practice administrator, treatment coordinator. Solo practices are owner-run. Group practices have administrative decision-makers.
