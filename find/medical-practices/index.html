Who Sells to Medical Practices

Medical practices are sophisticated buyers with specific needs. They purchase clinical equipment, technology, professional services, and operational support. Different products reach different decision-makers within practices.

Medical equipment and supplies. Clinical equipment, exam tables, diagnostic devices, disposable supplies. Physicians influence clinical purchases. Office managers handle supplies and consumables.

EHR and practice management software. Electronic health records, scheduling, billing, patient communication. Major technology decisions are made by physicians and administrators together. These are sticky purchases.

Revenue cycle management. Billing services, coding, claims management, denial recovery. Practice administrators control these decisions. The complexity of medical billing drives outsourcing.

Staffing and recruiting. Physicians, nurses, medical assistants, administrative staff. Healthcare has chronic staffing shortages. Practice managers and HR handle recruiting.

Marketing services. Patient acquisition, reputation management, website development. The physician owner or practice administrator decides. Most practices want more patients but don't know how to market.

Financial services. Practice loans, equipment financing, merchant services, retirement plans. Independent practice owners need these like other business owners.

Compliance and credentialing. HIPAA compliance, credentialing services, regulatory consulting. Required activities that practices often outsource.

Medical Practice Segments

Solo practitioners. One physician practices. The physician is the owner and decision-maker. Quick decisions but limited budgets.

Small group practices (2-5 physicians). Shared overhead and resources. May have dedicated administrator. Partners typically decide together.

Large group practices (6+ physicians). More formal structure with administrative leadership, IT support, and purchasing processes.

Multi-specialty groups. Multiple specialties under one roof. Complex decision-making with specialty-specific and organizational needs.

By ownership. Independent physician-owned practices operate differently from practices owned by health systems or PE groups. Independent practices have more purchasing autonomy.

By specialty. Primary care (family, internal, pediatrics) vs specialists. Each specialty has different needs, procedures, and purchasing patterns.
