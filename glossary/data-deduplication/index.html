Data Deduplication: How to Find and Merge Duplicate Records

Data deduplication is the process of identifying duplicate records in a CRM or database and merging them into single, accurate entries. It involves matching records by shared fields like email, phone, or company name, then consolidating the best data from each duplicate into one surviving record while discarding the rest.

If you've ever pulled a report and noticed the same person listed three times with slightly different names, you already know the problem. Duplicates don't just clutter your database. They actively mislead your team. Pipeline looks bigger than it is. Reps step on each other's accounts. Marketing blasts the same person twice in one week. The downstream effects compound fast.

Why Duplicates Form in the First Place

Duplicates rarely happen because someone made a mistake. They happen because of how modern CRMs collect data from multiple sources simultaneously.

CSV imports without dedup checks

Someone uploads a conference attendee list or a purchased contact list. The CRM creates new records for everyone, even if half of them already exist. Most import tools don't check for duplicates by default, or they only match on exact email, which misses a lot.

Web forms creating new contacts

A prospect fills out your demo form using their work email. Three months later they download a whitepaper using a personal Gmail. That's two records for one person, and your CRM has no way to know they're the same human.

Integration sync issues

Your marketing automation platform syncs contacts to your CRM. Your sales engagement tool does the same. Your support ticketing system pushes customer data over. Each integration creates records independently. If the email format differs even slightly between systems, you get duplicates.

Manual entry by sales reps

A rep meets someone at a trade show and creates a new contact. They don't search first, or they search by last name and don't find "Bob" because the existing record says "Robert." Now you've got two records that'll live side by side until someone catches it.

Most databases accumulate duplicates at a rate of 10‑30% over time. The more data sources feeding into the system, the faster the rate climbs.

Types of Duplicate Matching

Not all duplicates are obvious. Finding them requires different matching strategies depending on how messy the data is.

Exact matching

The simplest approach. Two records share an identical value in a key field like email address, phone number, or company domain. If john@acme.com appears on two contact records, that's a match. Exact matching is fast and reliable, but it only catches the easy ones.

Fuzzy matching

This is where it gets interesting. Fuzzy matching uses algorithms to score how similar two field values are, even when they're not identical. "Acme Corporation" and "Acme Corp" aren't an exact match, but a fuzzy algorithm like Jaro‑Winkler or Levenshtein distance will flag them as a probable duplicate. Same with "123 Main St" vs "123 Main Street." Fuzzy matching catches the bulk of duplicates that exact matching misses.

Phonetic matching

Algorithms like Soundex and Metaphone encode names by how they sound rather than how they're spelled. "Smith" and "Smyth" get the same phonetic code. "Catherine" and "Katherine" do too. Phonetic matching is especially useful for name fields where the same person might be entered with different spellings.

Composite matching

The most effective dedup processes combine multiple match types. You might run exact matching on email, fuzzy matching on company name, and phonetic matching on contact name, then score each potential duplicate pair based on how many criteria they hit. A match on email alone is high confidence. A match on name plus company plus city, but different email, is still worth reviewing.

The Deduplication Process, Step by Step

Whether you're cleaning up 5,000 records or 500,000, the process follows the same basic sequence.

Step 1: Export and backup. Before you touch anything, pull a full export of your database. Store it somewhere safe. Dedup operations involve deleting records, and you want a safety net if something goes wrong.

Step 2: Define your match keys. Decide which fields to match on. Email is the strongest single key for contacts. For companies, domain name works well. You'll typically use a combination: email + last name, or company domain + company name.

Step 3: Run matching. Process your records through exact, fuzzy, and phonetic matching. This produces a list of potential duplicate pairs, each with a confidence score.

Step 4: Review and confirm. High‑confidence pairs (exact email match) can usually be auto‑merged. Lower‑confidence pairs need a human to look at them. "John Smith at Acme" and "J. Smith at Acme Inc" are probably the same person. "John Smith at Acme" and "John Smith at Apex" probably aren't.

Step 5: Merge. For each confirmed duplicate set, designate one record as the master. Pull in any non‑empty fields from the duplicate. Transfer activity history, notes, deals, and tasks to the surviving record. Then delete the duplicate.

Step 6: Set up prevention. Dedup is pointless if you don't stop new duplicates from forming. Configure duplicate detection rules in your CRM, add validation logic to web forms, and establish import procedures that include a dedup check.

Common Mistakes That Make Dedup Worse

Deduplication sounds straightforward. But the ways it goes wrong are surprisingly consistent.

Merging without reviewing. Auto‑merging everything above a certain confidence threshold sounds efficient until you merge two different people named "David Chen" who happen to work at companies with similar names. False positives in dedup destroy real data. Always human‑review anything below a high‑confidence threshold.

Losing activity history. The whole point of merging is to get a complete picture. If your merge process deletes the duplicate without transferring its notes, emails, tasks, and deal associations to the surviving record, you've lost context your team needs. Check that your merge logic handles associated objects, not just field values.

Deduping once and calling it done. A one‑time cleanup is nice. But if you don't fix the sources creating duplicates, you'll be back in the same spot in six months. Prevention rules, import checks, and form validation need to go in alongside the cleanup.

Matching on the wrong fields. Using first name + last name as your primary match key will generate massive false‑positive rates. There are a lot of people named "Mike Johnson" in the world. Match keys need to be specific enough to minimize false positives while broad enough to catch real duplicates. Email plus name is a better starting point than name alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many duplicate records does a typical CRM have?

Most B2B CRMs have between 10% and 30% duplicate records. The rate depends on how many data sources feed into the system, whether duplicate‑prevention rules are in place, and how long the database has been accumulating records. Databases that pull from multiple integrations, web forms, and manual entry tend to be on the higher end.

What's the difference between exact matching and fuzzy matching?

Exact matching finds records where a specific field is identical, like two contacts sharing the same email address. Fuzzy matching catches near‑duplicates where the data is similar but not identical, like "Robert Smith" versus "Bob Smith" or "Acme Corp" versus "Acme Corporation." Fuzzy matching uses algorithms like Levenshtein distance, Jaro‑Winkler, or phonetic encoding (Soundex, Metaphone) to score how similar two values are.

Can I deduplicate my CRM without losing data?

Yes, but it requires careful merge logic. The standard approach is to designate one record as the "master" and pull in any non‑empty fields from the duplicate before deleting it. Activity history, notes, and associated deals should transfer to the surviving record. Always export a backup before running any merge operation, and test your merge rules on a small batch first.

How often should I deduplicate my database?

Run a full deduplication pass at least quarterly. If your database has high‑volume inbound sources like web forms, event imports, or third‑party integrations, monthly is better. The real fix is combining periodic bulk dedup with real‑time prevention rules that catch duplicates at the point of entry.

Should I deduplicate manually or use a service?

For databases under 5,000 records, manual dedup in your CRM's built‑in tools is often sufficient. Above that, the volume of potential matches makes manual review impractical. A professional service can process tens of thousands of records in hours, apply consistent merge logic, and set up prevention rules so the problem doesn't come back.
