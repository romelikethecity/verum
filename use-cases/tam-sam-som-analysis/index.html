The TAM SAM SOM Problem

You're preparing for a board meeting or investor pitch. Someone asks about your Total Addressable Market. So you pull numbers from a Gartner report or Grand View Research study that says your market is worth $47 billion by 2027.

The problem? That number tells you almost nothing useful.

Industry reports use top-down estimates. They take total industry revenue, apply growth assumptions, and produce a number that sounds impressive but has no connection to your actual business. A company selling cybersecurity compliance software to mid-market healthcare organizations is not competing for the entire $47 billion "healthcare IT market." They're competing for a specific slice of companies that match their product, pricing, and go-to-market capabilities.

The Inflation Problem

Most TAM calculations are inflated by 5x to 10x or more. Founders and strategy teams grab the largest defensible number because bigger sounds better. But experienced investors see through this immediately. According to research from First Round Capital, unrealistic market sizing is one of the most common reasons VCs pass on otherwise promising deals.1

When you claim a $50 billion TAM without showing your methodology, you're signaling one of two things: either you don't understand your market well enough to size it accurately, or you're deliberately inflating numbers to make your opportunity look bigger. Neither inspires confidence.

The Assumption Problem

Top-down estimates rely on assumptions that rarely hold. The Gartner report assumes certain growth rates, adoption curves, and market dynamics. Your business operates under different constraints. You have a specific ideal customer profile, geographic focus, pricing model, and competitive positioning. None of that shows up in an industry-wide estimate.

The result is a number that looks good in a slide deck but provides zero strategic value. You can't build a territory plan around "the healthcare IT market is $47 billion." You can't forecast pipeline around a percentage of an imaginary total.

What Investors and Boards Actually Want

Smart investors and board members don't want to hear that you're going after a trillion-dollar market. They want to see that you understand your market with precision. They want bottom-up analysis that shows you've done the work to identify who actually buys what you sell.

According to Andreessen Horowitz's market sizing guide, the most credible TAM analyses are built bottom-up from actual customer data and company counts.2 Instead of saying "the market is $47 billion and we'll capture 1%," you say "there are 12,400 companies that match our ICP, with an average contract value of $45,000, giving us a SAM of $558 million."

One approach sounds like guessing. The other sounds like you know your business.

Understanding TAM, SAM, and SOM

Before diving into methodology, let's define these terms clearly. They're often confused or used interchangeably, but they measure fundamentally different things.

TAM: Total Addressable Market

TAM represents everyone who could theoretically buy your product if you had unlimited resources, no competition, and perfect market conditions. It's the ceiling, not the target. For a B2B software company, TAM might be every company in your target industry regardless of size, geography, or current technology stack.

TAM answers: "How big is the entire opportunity space?"

Example: A company selling HR software might have a TAM of all 6.1 million employer businesses in the United States, multiplied by average contract value. That's the theoretical maximum if every single business bought your product.

SAM: Serviceable Addressable Market

SAM narrows the focus to companies you can realistically serve given your business model, geography, pricing, and capabilities. It's TAM filtered by your actual constraints. If you only sell to mid-market companies (100-1,000 employees) in the United States, your SAM excludes enterprises, SMBs, and international markets.

SAM answers: "Of the total market, which portion could we actually reach?"

Example: The same HR software company, targeting only mid-market companies in the US that currently use legacy systems, might have a SAM of 47,000 companies. That's a much more useful number for planning.

SOM: Serviceable Obtainable Market

SOM represents what you can realistically capture in the near term, typically 1-3 years. It accounts for competition, sales capacity, brand awareness, and market penetration rates. Even if 47,000 companies fit your ICP, you're not going to win all of them. SOM is your realistic target.

SOM answers: "What can we actually win in the next 1-3 years?"

Example: With 5 sales reps, reasonable win rates, and established competitors, that HR software company might realistically target a SOM of 2,000 companies, or about 4% of their SAM. That's the number that drives hiring plans and revenue forecasts.

How Verum Calculates TAM SAM SOM

We use bottom-up methodology with actual company data. Instead of multiplying percentages and assumptions, we count real companies and apply your specific criteria.

Step 1: Define TAM Criteria

We start by identifying every company that could theoretically buy your product. This means defining:

  • Industry classification using NAICS, SIC, or custom taxonomy
  • Geographic scope down to state, metro, or country level
  • Company type (private, public, nonprofit, government)
  • Basic size thresholds (minimum revenue or employee count)

We then pull every company matching these criteria from our database of 50+ sources, giving you an actual count. Not an estimate. A list of companies.

Step 2: Filter to SAM

Next, we apply your ICP characteristics to narrow the list to companies you can realistically serve:

  • Employee count ranges that match your pricing and product fit
  • Revenue thresholds that align with your contract values
  • Technology stack requirements if integration matters
  • Specific industry sub-segments where you have traction
  • Exclusion criteria (companies too small, too large, wrong vertical)

This produces your SAM: the actual number of companies that fit your model. And because it's built from real data, you can see the list. You know exactly who's in your addressable market.

Step 3: Calculate SOM

For SOM, we apply realistic market capture assumptions based on:

  • Your current market penetration (existing customers as % of SAM)
  • Sales capacity constraints (reps, territory coverage, deal cycles)
  • Competitive intensity in different segments
  • Historical win rates against different competitor types
  • Growth trajectory and expansion plans

The output is a defensible near-term target that you can actually use for planning. It's not a fantasy number; it's what you can realistically pursue given your resources.

Why TAM SAM SOM Accuracy Matters

For Fundraising

Investors pattern-match against thousands of pitches. When they see inflated market numbers, they discount everything else you say. A credible, bottom-up TAM SAM SOM analysis signals that you understand your market deeply. It's the difference between "we're going after a huge market" and "we've identified 8,200 organizations that fit our ICP and here's how we'll capture 4% in year one."

For Board Reporting

Board members want to understand market opportunity relative to your penetration. If your SAM is 8,200 companies and you have 180 customers, your board knows you're at 2.2% penetration with significant room to grow. That's a strategic conversation. "The market is $47 billion" is not.

For Go-to-Market Planning

You can't plan territories around imaginary numbers. When you know there are exactly 2,400 target companies in the Northeast and 1,800 in the West, you can allocate reps accordingly. Real company counts drive real planning.

For Product Strategy

Understanding your SAM composition reveals product opportunities. If 60% of your SAM uses a specific technology stack, that integration becomes a priority. If 40% are in a sub-vertical you haven't targeted, there's expansion potential. The data tells you where to focus.
