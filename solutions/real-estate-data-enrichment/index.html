The Real Estate Data Problem

It's a slow Tuesday morning. Your brokerage's top producer decides to finally work that database of past clients. She knows there's gold in there. People who closed three years ago and might be ready to sell. Past leads who weren't ready but might be now.

She opens the CRM. First ten calls: four disconnected numbers, two wrong people entirely, one who's annoyed she's calling again, and three who actually pick up. Two of those three already listed with someone else. One says maybe next year.

Three hours in, she's made 40 calls and had exactly two real conversations. The database has 2,000 contacts. At this rate, she'd need months to work through it. She closes the laptop and goes back to open houses.

This happens constantly in real estate. Agents collect contact data for years, but when they actually need it, half of it doesn't work.

CRM adoption sounds good on paper

About 72.5% of real estate professionals use CRM software now. That number has grown from 56% in recent years. But adoption isn't the same as effectiveness. Research puts CRM failure rates between 18% and 69%, depending on how you measure it. Getting the tool is easy. Getting value from it is another story.

The data decay problem is relentless

Contact data decays at roughly 25% per year. People move. Phone numbers change. Email addresses become obsolete. In real estate, the turnover is even faster. That lead from two years ago? There's a one-in-four chance their contact info is already wrong. Multiply that across thousands of records and you've got a database full of dead ends.

Agents collect data but don't use it

Most real estate agents collect a lot of client information but do little to turn it into actual sales. The difference between a top producer and everyone else often comes down to how they mine this data. But mining takes time. And when the data is bad, that time is wasted.

More than 40% of CRM features go unused

Most businesses use fewer than half of the available CRM features. It's not because the features aren't useful. It's because the underlying data is too messy to make them work. Lead scoring doesn't help when half your leads have wrong phone numbers. Automated follow-ups don't help when emails bounce.

The math is simple. Bad data means wasted calls, bounced emails, and missed opportunities. Your team deserves better than spending half their day verifying information that should already be clean.
