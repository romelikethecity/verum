The Travel Data Problem

Conference season is here. You're reaching out to corporate travel managers about group bookings and negotiated rates. Great timing. These conversations should be happening.

Except the travel manager at target company #1 was laid off last year. Company #2 shifted all booking authority to an executive assistant who isn't in your system. Company #3 changed their travel policy to use a managed travel company, and your direct contact no longer makes decisions. Your outreach is landing with people who can't say yes anymore.

Corporate travel structures change constantly. Your data doesn't keep up.

Travel management roles are unstable

Corporate travel management isn't always a dedicated position. Sometimes it's handled by procurement. Sometimes by HR. Sometimes by executive assistants. Sometimes by a managed travel service. The role shifts, gets combined with other responsibilities, or gets outsourced. Your contact from last year might not be doing travel anymore.

Post-pandemic restructuring changed everything

Travel policies got rewritten. Teams got restructured. Some companies brought travel management in-house. Others outsourced it. The decision-making structure for corporate travel at most companies looks nothing like it did a few years ago. Your database still reflects the old world.

Traveler contact data fragments across touchpoints

Loyalty program data. Booking history. Meeting planner contacts. Group sales records. Each system holds different information about the same people, often with conflicting details. When a frequent traveler changes jobs, some systems know, others don't.

Corporate account relationships need constant maintenance

Negotiated rates. Preferred vendor status. RFP cycles. These relationships require ongoing contact with the right people. But the people change. New travel manager. New procurement director. New executive assistant handling the CEO's travel. One turnover can undo months of relationship building.

Every email to someone who changed roles is a booking you didn't get. Every pitch to a travel manager who no longer makes decisions is time wasted. Every corporate account where you don't know the current structure is revenue going to competitors. In a market this size, stale data costs real money.
