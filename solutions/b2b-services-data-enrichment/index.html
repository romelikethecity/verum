The B2B Services Data Problem

It's Monday morning. You need to pull together a client review for your biggest account. Revenue numbers, project history, key contacts, upcoming renewals. Should be straightforward.

Your CRM says the main contact is Jennifer, VP of Operations. Your project management tool has three different Jennifers, none with VP titles. Your billing system shows invoices going to someone named Michael who you've never heard of. The company name is spelled differently in each system.

Two hours later, you've cross-referenced everything manually and built a spreadsheet you're still not confident about. The client meeting is in an hour.

Client data lives in too many places

CRM, project management, billing, time tracking, email. Every system has a version of your client data, and none of them agree. The CRM was updated by sales, the project tool by delivery, the billing system by finance. Nobody owns the single source of truth because there isn't one.

Contact decay hits service businesses hard

Research shows 30% of B2B contacts go stale every year. For service businesses built on relationships, that's devastating. The client champion who loved your work got promoted. The decision-maker who signed your last contract moved to a competitor. Your carefully built relationship maps are decaying while you watch.

Cross-sell and upsell analysis takes forever

You know there's more business in your existing clients. But identifying which ones might need additional services requires pulling data from five systems, normalizing it, and hoping the analysis is right. By the time you've done it for one client, you've burned half a day.

Client turnover creates onboarding nightmares

When your client's team changes, you need to rebuild relationships fast. But your contact data doesn't tell you who the new stakeholders are, who has authority, or who you should be talking to. You're starting cold with a warm account.

Every hour spent reconciling data across systems is an hour not spent serving clients. Every wrong email is a relationship signal missed. Your team deserves better than a data archaeology expedition every time they need basic client information.
