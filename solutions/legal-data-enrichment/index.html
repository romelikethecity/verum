The Law Firm Data Problem

It's Friday afternoon. A partner is preparing for a pitch meeting next week. Big potential client in the pharmaceutical industry. She knows the firm has done work in this space before, knows there are relationships to leverage.

She opens the CRM. Three different records for the same company, each with different contacts. One shows a GC who retired two years ago. Another has an email that bounces. The third has a phone number that goes to a fax machine. She spends an hour on LinkedIn trying to figure out who actually works there now.

This happens constantly at law firms. The CRM was supposed to make business development easier. Instead, it's become a graveyard of outdated information that nobody trusts.

Your CRM data is mostly incomplete

According to industry research, 90% of client data in law firm CRMs is incomplete. Missing phone numbers. Outdated titles. Email addresses that haven't worked in years. The data exists in fragments across the system, but nobody has stitched it together.

Attorneys have given up on the system

Only 29% of attorneys use the CRM consistently. The rest have stopped trusting it. When the data is wrong more often than it's right, why bother checking? They fall back on personal Rolodexes, Outlook contacts, and memory. All that institutional knowledge stays locked in individual heads instead of being shared across the firm.

Contact decay is relentless

Research shows that 30% of contact data degrades every year. General counsels move companies. In-house lawyers get promoted. Companies merge, rebrand, relocate. Your CRM doesn't update itself. Every month that passes, your data gets a little more stale.

The cost of fixing it reactively is enormous

Studies show that fixing bad data costs 10 times more than preventing it in the first place. Every hour a partner spends researching contacts is an hour not spent on billable work or actual business development. Gartner puts the average cost of bad data at $12.9 million annually across industries. For law firms, that shows up in missed opportunities, embarrassing outreach, and duplicated effort.

The irony is that most firms know their data is bad. The 2024 CRM Success Survey found firms rated their data quality at just 6 out of 10. But knowing it's bad and fixing it are two different things. Nobody has time to clean up decades of accumulated records.
