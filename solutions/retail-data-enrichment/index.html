The Retail Data Problem

It's line review season. Your sales team is scheduling meetings with retail buyers across your key accounts. The relationships you've spent years building. The placements that drive the majority of your revenue.

Except the buyer at your biggest account switched categories. The category manager at chain #2 left for a competitor. The VP of Merchandising at chain #3 retired and you're back to square one with their replacement. Your carefully maintained buyer relationships are suddenly full of question marks.

Retail moves fast. Your data isn't keeping up.

Retail turnover is extraordinary

Retail has some of the highest turnover rates of any industry. At the buyer and category manager level, people move between retailers, switch categories, get promoted, or leave the industry entirely. The buyer you pitched last season might not be in that seat today.

Category assignments change without notice

Retailers reorganize. Categories get combined or split. The buyer who handled your product line might now be responsible for something completely different. The new buyer handling your category might not know who you are.

Corporate versus store contacts create complexity

Selling to retail often means managing relationships at multiple levels. Corporate buyers, regional merchandisers, store-level managers. Each level has different data quality challenges. A contact database that mixes all of them creates chaos.

Retail consolidation reshapes your market

Retailers merge. Chains get acquired. Stores close. The retail landscape shifts constantly, and your account data doesn't automatically reflect those changes. You might be pitching accounts that no longer exist in their original form.

Every email to someone who changed roles is a relationship at risk. Every pitch to the wrong category buyer is a meeting that won't happen. Every account you don't know was acquired is a sale that might slip away. In a competitive CPG landscape, you can't afford to be working from last year's org charts.
