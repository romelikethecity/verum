The Insurance Data Problem

It's renewal season. Your agency has 3,000 policies coming up in the next 60 days. You need to reach every one of those clients, confirm coverage needs haven't changed, and prevent them from shopping around.

Your producer pulls the list. First 50 calls: eight disconnected numbers, twelve voicemails to addresses that sound wrong, six people who say they already talked to someone else at your agency, and maybe fifteen actual conversations. Some of those conversations reveal the client moved to a new state six months ago and their policy doesn't even apply anymore.

The data exists. It's just scattered, duplicated, and decaying faster than anyone can fix it manually.

AI adoption is up, but data quality isn't

According to Conning research, 77% of insurance companies now use AI in their operations, up from 61% the year before. Chatbots handle 70% of routine inquiries. Predictive analytics drive underwriting decisions. But all of that sophistication falls apart when the underlying contact data is wrong.

Duplicate records cost real money

Operational inefficiencies from data errors cost insurers millions annually. Duplicate policyholder records, incorrect addresses, outdated phone numbers. Each error creates manual work, delays claims, or sends renewal notices into the void. The Coalition of Insurance Fraud estimates $80 billion is lost annually to fraudulent claims alone, and poor data quality makes detection harder.

Agent data fragmentation is endemic

Insurance agents struggle to compile data from different sources. Your agency management system has one version of the truth. Your quoting platform has another. Your marketing automation tool has a third. Getting a complete picture of a client relationship means pulling data from multiple places and hoping it matches.

Retention depends on clean data

According to McKinsey, satisfied policyholders are 80% more likely to renew. But you can't satisfy a policyholder if you can't reach them. Research shows CRM adoption improves customer retention by 27%, but only when the data in the CRM is actually accurate.

Every wrong phone number is a missed renewal call. Every bounced email is a client who might shop around without you knowing. Your producers deserve better than spending half their day verifying contact information.
